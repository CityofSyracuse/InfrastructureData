City of Syracuse

Data of Infrastructure

Much of the City of Syracuse's infrastructure has aged past its useful life. In order to tackle the issues in the most efficient and effective ways, the City is using data to documents and prioritize projects.

Infrastructure and the economy

Infrastructure is the backbone of the economy. It is the roads people commute on to get to work and the pipes that transport water so businesses can open. In Syracuse, when infrastructure fails, water main breaks shut down restaurants, prevent people from taking showers, and destroy roads. In New York City, sometimes people have to be evacuated from the subway following a water main break.

Proactive

The City of Syracuse is good at reacting to infrastructure problems. Most water main breaks are fixed in around eight hours, and potholes that have been complained about are filled in about six days. Reacting to infrastructure issues, though, means the problems will continue, and the City will not do enough to get to the problems before they happen.

Proactive solutions are critical, but difficult. Thousands of potholes appear each year in Syracuse, and water mains are underground, so it is difficult to assess risk.

This is where data can help us.

Potholes

Until 2016, the City of Syracuse only tracked when potholes were complained about, and when those specific potholes were filled. The department of public works (DPW) fills many more potholes, they just weren't tracked.

Additionally, DPW rated the quality of roads every year. This was done on a 1-10 scale, and while the rating system worked well, it was somewhat subjective since a person was doing the ratings.

Finding ways to track all the potholes filled, and automating the collection of road quality ratings would help to make operations more proactive. GPS units were put on all DPW vehicles, and each time the pothole filling machines filled a pothole, a row of data was logged to track the time and location of that pothole. Immediately, the City was tracking thousands of potholes being filled.

To supplement the road rating process, the City is now working with a company called Argo Labs and using their product, SQUID. IT is a low-cost computer, attached to a vehicle, that measures the bumpiness of a road using an accelerometer, and photographs the street with an attached camera. Logging the hundreds of miles of roads gives more information about the quality of the road.

Water Mains

Identifying risk with water mains is hard because the pipes are below the ground, so we cannot see them on a daily basis. Assessing them requires expensive equipment and staff-time.

Using data, we can use the history of water main breaks, and the attributes of water mains, to inform which pipes might be most risky in the future.

Partnering with the Eric & Wendy Schmidt Data Science for Social Good Summer Fellowship, we worked to build a predictive model that tells us which water mains have the highest risk of breaking in the next three years. If we chose 50 blocks in the city at random, we would expect about 5 of them to have a water main break on them by the end of 2018. Using this model, the top 50 riskiest blocks in the city should have 32 water main breaks on them by the end of 2018.

The map below shows where the top 50 (red) and top 500 (yellow) riskiest blocks are in the city. The blue dots show where water main breaks have happened so far this year. The model is not perfect, but it gives us better intelligence about our water mains at a full city-scale than ever before.

Knowing where water mains are riskiest is not just important for City operations, but it impacts the residents and businesses in the city. The map below shows the riskiest 50 blocks of water mains, as well as the buildings on each of those blocks. When a water main break happens, a restaurant will be forced to close, or a family won't be able to use water to cook, or shower. Using data to help operations ultimately saves money and hassle for the city and its residents.
